Music Through The Pandemic

Duration: July-November 2021

Engagement Type: College Project

Team Size: 2

Checkout:

  1. Website

My role: ..............

Objective

As a part of the Data Visualisation Course(761) at University of Maryland, we set out to discover using data

What effect did pandemic on the music people listen.

Deliverables

  1. Data Visualisation
  2. Website containing the narrative and visualisation

Process

We were free to choose the topic for exploration. Towards the starting, we realised both of us love music and we started to explore different ideas for the project. Few of the ideas we proposed –

  1. how have different music genres’ popularities changed over the years?
  2. Is there a way to see how the Beatles have influenced the music industry?

During one of our discussions, we realised that during the past 2 years, pandemic made us stay home. As a result, we listened to more music than ever. Thinking about the relationship between the pandemic and music, we got curious — did the pandemic affect the kinds of music we listened to?

The initial hypothesis we had was that people would have listened to more depressing and sad music during the pandemic as compared to pre-pandemic times. Accordingly, our process for the project had four steps:

  1. Defining the problem
  2. Ideation — Sketching, literature review, and exploring technology aspect
  3. Prototyping
  4. Evaluation with user

Ideation

We used Spotify charts data to extract the top 20 weekly songs between 2017 to 2021. To separate the dataset into two parts, we considered two periods -

1) Pre-pandemic: 2017 to mid 2020

2) Post-pandemic: mid 2020 to present.

Next, we tried to come up with a way of finding out the emotions of each song programmatically. It was impossible for us to go through all the songs from 2017 to 2021 and define their emotions. We used Spotify to solve this problem as well. Spotify provides psycho-acoustic parameters(energy, valence, acousticness, danceability, etc used to define a song) . These parameters are used by Spotify in its recommendation engines. We used the energy and valence parameters from the Spotify API along with Russell’s Circumplex model to define emotions for all the songs we extracted from Spotify charts. Russell’s Circumplex model is discussed more in the “Exploring literature” section.

Research for Ideas

We explored music visualisations articles posted on the New York Times, Pinterest, and Tableau community. Among all, radar plots representing acoustic features of summer hit songs since 1988 from New York Times was the most impressive work. Radar plots effectively show that acoustic features of summer hit songs have been following a certain pattern and consequently, most of them sound similar. Since we are using the same solution — Spotify API, we figured out that we can discover something interesting using multiple acoustic features provided by Spotify.

Then, we read academic papers related to music visualization. We discovered the paper “Seeing Sound” written by Cheong Un Lee. The author explains how she visualized the emotion of a song based on color-coded Ruselle’s emotion model. According to Russell’s model, emotion can be defined by calculating the correlation between Arousal and Valence values. Since “Energy” which is one of the Spotify acoustic features corresponds to the “Arousal”, we aimed to define the emotion of popular songs by mapping Spotify acoustic features to Russell’s model.

Sketches

Based on the literature review and knowledge about the available data, we started sketching different ideas for the project. Here are a few of the ideas we came up with.

To come up with the final design of the solution, we drew two rough sketches of possible visual representations. First, we designed a table chart presenting each emotion of weekly top charted songs. Each row represents a single week from 2017 to 2021 and each column represents 16 kinds of different emotions. Visualizing song moods by week makes it users easy to read how the emotion of popular songs has changed over time, however, it is difficult to read how pandemic affected the mood of songs people are listening to. To highlight the difference between pre-pandemic and post-pandemic, we created two circular color palettes. Every song is presented as a bubble and the bubble is color-coded according to the mood of the song. Compared to the first version of the sketch, users are more likely to easily compare what kinds of emotions were prevalent during two different eras.

By drawing two different visualizations, we were able to compare and contrast two possible solutions. Since our visualization is based on a large set of data, we wanted to focus on the legibility of visualization to provide viewers with an easy way to navigate data. Although it is easier for viewers to navigate data week by week with the first solution, the second solution is more effective to highlight the answer for the problem we are trying to solve. Moreover, circular palettes filled with multiple colored bubbles would provide an attractive user experience to viewers.

Prototype and Test

Our final goal was to create a website that took users through a narrative journey and then presented them with the visualization. Before then, first, we wanted to test and learn about the issues in our narrative and data visualization. We didn’t want to invest time in creating a website at this point. So, we created PowerPoint slides that contained the narrative for visualization. Then we decided to test the project with users, by first showing them the narrative slides and then showing the visualization with Tableau, with minimum moderation. We performed an evaluation with 3 users. For each of the users:

  1. During the evaluation: We observed each user going through the flow and noted down all the places where they struggled.
  2. After each evaluation: We asked users some questions to understand if they were able to understand the narrative and visualization — What kind of song mood was the most prevalent in the pre-pandemic? Which of the three emotions had the greatest change in proportion? Did you understand the way we found out the mood of the songs? What conclusion did you draw from the visualization? Any general feedback on the visualization?

We had the following learnings based on the evaluation -

1. Reorder the visualizations on the dashboard — Across the three interviews, there was at least one visualization (from bubble chart, data table, and pie chart) that people didn’t read. The visualization interface didn’t help users to navigate between different visualizations.

2. Help people with bubble charts — Bubble-packed charts are not as popular as a pie-chart or data tables, especially for data visualization novices. Even though users found them fascinating, they had difficulty understanding them. Thus, we might need to provide some additional instruction to help users with reading them.

3. Minor issues — During each evaluation, we encountered multiple minor issues. We took some time between subsequent interviews and fixed these minor issues. This helped us to get more valuable feedback with every interview. These minor fixes are explained in detail here.

Conclusion

From two pie charts representing pre-pandemic and post-pandemic, we discovered something very unexpected. We assumed that people would more likely to listen to depressing songs during the pandemic, however, according to the percentage comparison table, the percentage of elated songs among top charted songs has increased more than two times. On the other hand, the percentage of stressed and upset songs has decreased significantly. This finding was the exact opposite of our initial hypothesis. The whole development process from data analysis to final release was not easy at all, however, we are thrilled to share what we have found while creating fascinating visualizations!
