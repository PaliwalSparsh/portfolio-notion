Music Through The Pandemic

Duration: July-November 2021

Engagement Type: Data Visualisation (761)

Team Size: 2

Objective

This project was a part of the Data Visualisation Course course at UMD. The goal of the project was to create a data visualisation which allows either discovery or presentation of information.

The project was done in a team of two. We were given the freedom to select the topic for the project.

Coming up with the idea

Both me and my teammate for the project share love for music. During one of our discussions, we talked about how we spent a large portion of our time during lockdown listening to music. One of the questions that came up during this discussion became our topic for exploration — did the pandemic affect the kinds of music we listened to?

Our initial hypothesis was that people listened to more depressing and sad music during the pandemic as compared to pre-pandemic times.

Process

Gathering data

We used the Spotify Weekly Hits from 2017 to 2021 for the project. These songs are ranked on the number of times they’ve been streamed. This enabled us to know what songs were listened the most each week in this time period.

Next, we tried to come up with a way of finding out the emotions of each song. This had to be done programmatically as it was not possible manually by listening to 2000+ songs. During our research, we found a way of converting features of a song (pyscho acoustic parameters) defined by Spotify into emotions using Russell’s Circumplex model.

Presenting the data

Since, we had finalised on the data, we started sketching different ideas for visualisation.

While exploring for inspiration, we came around an amazing music visualisation by New York Times. Here, they used radar plots to represent the variations and similarities in acoustic features of summer hit songs since 1988. We loved the narrative nature of the article and decided that the visualisation we are working on will be a part of a webpage that containing supplemental narrative.

Prototype and Test

We developed the visualisation in Tableau and prototyped the narrative using slides. Then, we performed an evaluation with 3 users. We observed how users interacted with the visualisation and reacted to the narrative. After each evaluation, we asked users some questions to understand if they were able to understand the visulization.

Based on the learnings we reordered elements in visualisation, added legends to charts, and made technical parts of narrative easier to read.

Outcome

Finally, we developed the website for the narrative and integrated the visualisation into it. You can check out the website here.

Here is a video of the visualisation part of the project.

Surprising findings

Through our exploration, we discovered something very unexpected. Our findings were exact opposite of our initial hypothesis. The percentage of elated songs in weekly charts increased more than two times. On the other hand, the percentage of stressed and upset songs decreased significantly.
